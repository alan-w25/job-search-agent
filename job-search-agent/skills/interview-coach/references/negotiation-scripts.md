# Negotiation Scripts & Frameworks

Specific language for every stage of salary negotiation, from first mention
through final counter-offer.

## Rule #1: Never Give a Number First

When asked about salary expectations early in the process, deflect:

**Script — Phone Screen Deflection:**
"I'd love to learn more about the role and the team before talking compensation.
Can you share the range budgeted for this position? That way I can tell you if
we're in the same ballpark."

**If they push:**
"I'm flexible and open to discussing this as we move forward. My priority is finding
the right fit. What's the range you have in mind for the right candidate?"

**If they really push (some companies require it):**
"Based on my research for this level and market, I'd be looking in the range of
$[X] to $[Y]. But I'm most interested in the total package and the opportunity itself."
→ X should be 10-15% above your actual target. Y should be aspirational but defensible.

## When You Receive an Offer

### Step 1: Express Enthusiasm, Buy Time

**Script:**
"Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this opportunity and the team. I'd love
to take [2-3 days / until Friday] to review the full package. Can you send the offer
details in writing so I can look at everything together?"

Never accept on the spot. Even if it's perfect. Reviewing shows professionalism
and gives you negotiation space.

### Step 2: Evaluate Before Responding

Calculate total compensation:
- Base salary
- Bonus (and historical payout — ask: "what was the average bonus payout last year?")
- Equity value (current valuation × shares × vesting period, minus strike price if options)
- Sign-on bonus (one-time, doesn't compound)
- Benefits monetary value (healthcare, 401k match, HSA contributions)

Compare against:
- Market data (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Blind, Payscale)
- Your current compensation
- Your salary floor from profile preferences
- Competing offers if any

### Step 3: Counter-Offer

**Script — Base Salary Counter:**
"I'm very excited about the role and the team. After reviewing the offer and
researching the market for [role level] in [market], I was hoping we could discuss
the base salary. Given my [X years] of experience in [specific relevant domain]
and the impact I can bring — particularly [reference specific value from interviews] —
I'd be more comfortable at $[target]. Is there flexibility there?"

**Key principles:**
- Always justify with specifics (experience, market data, unique skills)
- Reference things discussed in interviews to reinforce your value
- Be collaborative, not adversarial: "I was hoping we could discuss" not "I demand"
- Give a specific number, not a range (ranges anchor to the bottom)

### Step 4: If They Say "This Is Our Best Offer"

**Script — Pivoting to Non-Salary Items:**
"I understand the base may be fixed. I'm still very interested in making this work.
Could we explore [one or more of the following]:
- A sign-on bonus to bridge the gap?
- Additional equity/RSU grant?
- An accelerated review cycle (6 months instead of 12) with a path to [target]?
- Extra PTO days?
- A one-time relocation/setup budget?
- A later start date to handle transition?"

**Priority order for non-salary negotiation:**
1. Sign-on bonus (immediate cash, easy for companies to approve)
2. Equity (often has more budget flexibility than base)
3. Accelerated review (gets you to your target number faster)
4. Title upgrade (costs them nothing, boosts your next negotiation)
5. PTO / flexibility (also costs little, high personal value)

### Step 5: The Competing Offer Lever

**If you have a real competing offer:**
"I want to be transparent — I'm also in the final stages with [Company]. Their offer
is [higher base / more equity / etc.]. I genuinely prefer your team and the role,
but I want to make sure the compensation reflects the market. Is there room to adjust?"

**Rules:**
- Only mention real offers. Fabricating is unethical and risky.
- Don't name-drop unless the competitor is equally or more prestigious
- Frame it as "I prefer you, help me choose you" — not as a threat
- Specific numbers are more credible than vague "another offer"

### Step 6: Accepting

**Script:**
"I'm thrilled to accept. Thank you for working with me on the compensation —
I really appreciate it. Could you send the updated offer letter? I'm excited
to get started on [start date]."

## Handling Specific Scenarios

**Lowball Offer (>20% below market):**
"Thank you for the offer. I'm excited about the role, but honestly the base salary
is significantly below market for this level. Based on [data source], the range for
[role] in [market] is $X-$Y. I'd need to be at least at $[number] to make this work.
Is there flexibility to get closer to market rate?"

**They Rescind After Negotiation (extremely rare but it happens):**
An employer who rescinds for reasonable negotiation is telling you something
important about their culture. You dodged a bullet. Move on.

**Verbal Offer Only:**
"I appreciate the verbal offer! Could you send the details in writing?
I want to make sure I'm looking at the complete picture."
→ Never negotiate or accept verbal-only offers.

**"Take It or Leave It" Pressure:**
"I appreciate the urgency. I want to make a thoughtful decision because I take
commitment seriously. Can I have until [reasonable date]?"
→ If they won't give you 48 hours, that's a red flag about how they'll treat you
as an employee.

## Emotional Management During Negotiation

- It's normal to feel uncomfortable negotiating. You're not being greedy —
  you're establishing your market value.
- Companies EXPECT negotiation. The first offer is almost never the best offer.
  Budget for the role is typically 10-20% above the initial number.
- Negotiation doesn't make them like you less. Hiring managers negotiate too.
  They respect it.
- The worst they can say is "no, this is firm." Then you decide based on the
  original offer. The risk is near-zero.
